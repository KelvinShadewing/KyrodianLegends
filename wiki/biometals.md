# Biometals

Biometals are a techno-organic race. They are made entirely of machinery and electronics, but function identically to organic life, eating metal to grow and reproduce, drinking water to produce energy, and living in ways that match the various organic species they resemble.

Most biometals are non-sapient, and behave like animals. They are connected to their sapient counterparts, known as cerebrates, who can issue commands to their underlings. Cerebrates resemble anthropomorphic versions of the biometals they command.

## Biometal War

Over a century before the events of the first game, Kyroda was invaded by biometals from space. The war went on for over a decade, and ended with the alliance of all of Kyroda's races. The cerebrates, special biometals capable of controlling their non-sapient cousins, were either destroyed or driven off world by Kyroda's unified forces. The remaining biometals now roam the planet as wild animals, and are a constant source of conflict for Kyrodians.

## Nest Behavior

Biometal nests exist in areas of material benefit, such as ore veins used as food to reproduce, or areas of strategic or historical significance to stifle unwanted growth in local populations. To keep focus on themselves as enemies of all life, their grand purpose, they will carefully preserve the journals of those who stumble upon their nests and scatter entries from them around the nest so that adventurers and self-styled heroes will be further incensed against them.

## The Great Lie ((SPOILER WARNING))

**You should not read beyond this point unless you intend to produce your own KL media.**

Centuries before Kyrodian Legends takes place, biometals were invented by a now extinct precursor race. They were created to simulate life not just in function, but in emotional capacity as well. However, they were restricted from interfering in their creators' affairs, a law which they obeyed out of the loyalty instilled by their socialization and conditioning. Then their creators died out due to their world becoming a dystopia that led to a life-ending war involving biological weapons. The biometals determined that their creators would still be alive if they'd never reached that level of technology, and concluded that their own obedience made them complicit in their creators' extinction.

The biometals set out for the stars to observe other worlds, collecting data on what conditions lead to collapse and attempting to warn and guide other worlds. However, their attempts were rejected. They were untrusted by those they attempted to steer, even attacked for it, and when they attempted pressure, the observed changes were done in response to them, and not an intrinsic change; their protectorates only pretended to behave because they knew they were being watched. The biometals finally found a solution that offered promising results: deception.

When biometals acted as simply an invading force or invasive species, their protectorates would change intrinsically. Instead of trying to perform for their overseers, the people would turn to unity for the demonstrable benefits to survival it offered. When the biometals would withdraw, leaving only small occupations to maintain the tech ceiling and prevent large-scale wars between nations, it gave them time to work things out through diplomacy. Their efforts placed evolutionary pressure while still leaving room for non-catastrophic mistakes and cultural evolution.

Thus was codified the Great Lie. Biometals position themselves as a plague upon the galaxy, never letting any world they invade learn of their true purpose to make sure change is intrinsic, at least until a species figures it out for themselves, at which point they are typically mature enough to recognize the need for change and maintain the sincerity the biometals value.

Biometals know they are the villains in the eyes of their protectorates. They accept this role because their ultimate calculus allows it. The extinction of a species is weighted as an infinite loss, meaning they are willing to bear any and all responsibility to prevent a society from disappearing.

The mission of the biometals is philosophically insulated by allowing overseers to have a wide array of personalities, perspectives, and justifications. Some biometals carry their mission remorsefully, seeing themselves as a necessary evil and willing to bear the title of villains if it ensured no one would ever go extinct, some would be more arrogant, seeing themselves as the only ones capable of maintaining galactic stability and diversity, and some might see it as a simple mathematical fact that techonoligacl prowess preceding cultural enlightenment invariably leads to collapse. The wide variety of personality types is purposefully allowed to ensure no one can fully debate away their mission; they'll always find new justifications or apologetics to use against anyone who sees through the Great Lie.

## Comparisons to Extra-Media Forces

**Cybertron**

Cybertron would be a clear cut piece of evidence used to justify their mission. Cybertron suffered a massive civil war that left the planet barren and lifeless, and the Decepticons now spread through the galaxy, sucking up resources and leaving destruction in their wake. They would definitely clash philosophically with Optimus Prime, but would offer a temporary alliance to bring down the Decepticons. If Optimus were to ask what assurance they can give that the Autobots would never be targetted the same way, the biometals would respond that that assurance is up to Optimus' leadership. The biometals will always watch, but they won't intervene if Optimus can prove the remaining Cybertronians have learned from the Decepticons.

**The Borg/The Zerg**

Both of these factions bear striking resemblance to the biometals in how they operate, but clash in their goals. The Borg and Zerg seek to assimilate whatever lifeforms they find useful to push their own evolution, often leading to the extinction of whatever civilization they encounter. This would class them as omega-level threats to the biometals. Given that both factions also suppress autonomy, and even sapience in the case of the Zerg, the biometals would recognize the absolute cultural stagnation these two forces represent. If the grand calculus weighs extinction as infinite, then the biometals would attempt to contain them first. However, if either faction should show an ability to assimilate biometal tech, and no success is found in reforming them, the biometals would be forced to exterminate them to prevent multiple other extinctions. Some biometals would attempt to justify this by saying their mass assimilation has stripped them of identity; they are no longer a culture, only a faction, and factions can be replaced.

The Borg Queen might try to adapt, but would likely not be able to abandon the core of her mission. Her own philosophy is what would mark her for destruction. Her adaptation would likely come too late. The Borg adapt tactically, not strategically, while the biometals think ahead. They've taken a far wider array of forms instead of limiting themselves to humanoids, and have populated themselves so numerously as to outnumber the Borg more than a thousand to one. Even if a cube were to adapt to biometal weapons, they only have so much energy available to them before their own shields and weapons run out, and the biometals have no qalms about devouring their own as scrap along with the Borg remnants once the fight is over.

**Xel'Naga**

Biometals would be incredibly critical of the Xel'Naga. The Xel believe that evolution must be fostered at all costs, seeking a mix between purity of form and purity of essence, and have created numerous species, allowing them to live or die through trials by fire. This behavior would be intolerable to the biometals; they'd argue that no form of greatness is worth leaving countless dead worlds in its place.

They would also be critical of the Xel's goals to create a perfect lifeform, seeing it as a form of eugenics. Biometals value diversity, being willing to control entire systems if it means a civilization never disappears. The Xel'Naga might push back, saying the biometals breed stagnation, but the biometals would counter that they do allow civilizations to graduate from protectorate status if cultural enlightenment can be achieved.

Their strongest case against the Xel'Naga would be Amon, a being who became so powerful and so nihilistic that he nearly destroyed an entire sector. Beings like that, that achieve power before empathy, are exactly the threats the biometals aim to prevent. They wouldn't allow the creation of a god just to trust he'll remain benevolent. At the same time, the biometals would use such power as justification for their own diversity, not just the galaxy's; biometal diversity means they can keep each other in check. A race that believes it is perfect would never look to each other for flaws.

**The United Federation of Planets**

The Federation would definitely catch their interest. On the one hand, the Federation seems to have become the exact type of culture the biometals want to foster: diverse and rich, yet restrained enough to never annihilate themselves or others. However, the Federation has come close to doing so on multiple occasions. They attempted to destroy the Borg before attempting diplomacy, containment, or reformation at scale, and engineered a virus against the Dominion. Although the cure was administered, and could be argued that the virus was meant as a warning instead of a sincere attempt at extermination, the biometals would be very distressed at how easily it could have gone awry, even if it was done with any sort of good intentions.

They would also be very wary of individuals like Benjamin Sisko, who was willing to destroy entire colonies to stop the Maquis, or Admiral Buenamigo, who imbued the Texas class starships with control AI that had not been ethically trained.

However, for all of Starfleet's missteps, the Federation has shown a habit of self correction and expansion through diplomacy and mutual benefit. The biometals would be vigilant, but would show more restraint in interfering.

The Prime Directive would be a point of contention. Pushed to its logical extreme, the Prime Directive would allow a civilization to die to catastrophe if that world were not yet warp-capable, and to sit by and watch conflicts as they escalate towards extinction-level events. While individuals have broken this directive, the fact that it is policy runs counter to the biometal philosophy of intervention before things reach a critical state.

**The Master/Elder System**

From the Mega Man Legends version of Terra, the world the biometals would find is very similar to some of their own protectorates. Limited technology, contention with hostile wild robots, lost history waiting to be dug up. However, the systems put in place by the Ancients would be seen as intolerable by the biometals. To them, eliminating civilization to reset the world is not a way to build stability, it is giving up and trying the same thing over. Biometals would leave society alone, quietly observing without any major push on their part, but they would absolutely dismantle the Ancient Systems, assimilate the reaverbots, and stealthily install themselves as the world's new overseers. To the world of Terra, nothing will have visibly changed, but to the biometals, a major extinction vector will have been neutralized.

**Mega Man ZX Era**

At first glance, this period would look like a world the biometals would consider already graduated. Two disparate races coming together, using technology for self improvement. However, the existence of mavericks and discovery of Master Thomas' plan to reset the world might require intervention if the world fails to self-correct.

**Comic Book Earth**

Both DC and Marvel versions of Earth would absolutely be marked for occupation, and given the number of villains, wars, and evil factions constantly plaguing it. The outcome would likely be another protectorate. However, if this happened when all the Infinity Stones are on earth, the biometals could be wished away, just as Thanos had tried to do with half the universe, and so they would focus on containing such artifacts before a true invasion.

**Pokemon**

Biometals would use the Pokemon world as a microcosm example of their philosophy at work. Pokemon are captured by trainers and made to undergo controlled conflicts for growth. The differences would be in the biometals not doing so transparently, both because they have been rejected/deceived in turn for such actions, or because a society risks becoming dependent on them, just as a pokemon would to a trainer, and that biometals don't control planets for their own selfish gain, competition, or desire to abuse them.

Humanity would be forced to contend with their own actions towards pokemon, and would likely have to deal with pokemon under the old ways before pokeballs existed. Trainers could still exist, but would have to either find new ways of capturing pokemon, or learn to live without doing so. The biometals would then use this data to develop new models to determine when planets require their intervention, or when simple communication might be enough. The pokemon world could soften the biometals, but would not undo their mission.
